REF-015 — Document divergence rather than force unification when consolidation isn't free

When two skill folders carry "shared" reference documents that have diverged over time, the default reflex is to consolidate them into a single source of truth. The v0.2.2 audit of `cuo/cpo/fr-create` and `cuo/cpo/fr-audit` proved that reflex is wrong by default. The four reference documents present in both skills (`HITL_PROTOCOL.md`, `UNTRUSTED_CONTENT.md`, `ANTI_FABRICATION.md`, `EU_AI_ACT_DECISION_TREE.md`) are NOT byte-identical between the two skills, and the divergence is intentional: each is tuned to its consumer's lifecycle phase (write-side vs. read-side, generate-time vs. validate-time).

Forcing consolidation in v0.2.2 would have required either (a) a single super-doc with conditional sections — risk twice as long, half as readable, OR (b) splitting into shared + per-skill local files — risk fragmentation that complicates updates. Neither clearly improves on the divergent state.

**The chosen path:** document the divergence proactively in `references/README.md` index files in both skills. The next reader doesn't waste cycles on "which copy is canonical?" and doesn't get tempted to force unify by accident. The decision to revisit consolidation is gated on a future signal: if the divergence grows or maintenance pain becomes evident.

How to use this memory

When you encounter "shared" docs that have diverged across consumers, ask: (1) is the divergence intentional? (2) is consolidation cheap? (3) what's the cost of leaving them divergent? If the answers are yes/no/low, document the divergence and move on. Don't burn time forcing unification that the codebase doesn't reward.

This is a counter to the more common "DRY" reflex. DRY applies cleanly when consolidation is cheap (single-line constants, single-purpose functions). It does NOT apply cleanly when documents are written for different audiences, lifecycle phases, or invocation contexts.
